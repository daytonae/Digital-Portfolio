Algorithmic Bias in AI Systems

Hampton University · Survey Research · AI Ethics · Data Analysis

This research investigated how algorithmic bias affects minority communities and the critical role that diversity plays in AI development teams. I designed and distributed two original surveys, one targeting AI/IT professionals and one targeting minority communities, to gather data on awareness, impact, and proposed strategies for reducing bias in AI systems.

Research Goal

To identify effective, community-informed approaches for reducing algorithmic bias and increasing inclusivity in artificial intelligence, gathering perspectives from both the builders and the communities most impacted.

Methodology

  • Designed two parallel surveys tailored to different audiences: AI/IT professionals and minority group members.
  • Distributed surveys across social media platforms, university networks, and professional communities.
  • Analyzed survey data to identify patterns in awareness, experience with bias, and recommendations for equitable AI design.
  • Synthesized findings into actionable strategies for reducing bias in AI development pipelines.

Key Findings

  • Significant gaps exist in awareness of algorithmic bias between technical and non-technical communities.
  • Diversity in AI development teams was consistently identified as a key factor in reducing bias.
  • Proposed strategies included diverse hiring pipelines, bias audits, and community feedback loops in model development.

Research · Capstone · Cybersecurity

Heroes or Villains: Media & Ethical Hacker Perceptions

Hampton University · Senior Capstone · Survey Research · Content Analysis · Tableau

This senior capstone study examines how media representations of hacking shape public awareness and trust in ethical hackers (penetration testers) compared to malicious hackers. Popular films, TV shows, and video games consistently portray hackers as criminals, blurring the line between white-hat and black-hat hacking, and eroding public trust in legitimate cybersecurity professionals.

Research Question

How do media representations of hacking influence public awareness and trust in ethical hackers versus malicious hackers?

Methodology

  • Conducted a comprehensive literature review on media influence, public perception, and ethical hacking.
  • Designed and distributed a public survey targeting everyday citizens aged 18+ across social media platforms.
  • Performed content analysis of mainstream media (TV, film, gaming) to identify recurring hacker stereotypes.
  • Visualized results using data visualization tools and presented findings verbally.

Key Findings

  • Media portrayals consistently link ethical hackers with criminal behavior, creating public mistrust.
  • Most participants lacked awareness of the distinction between ethical and malicious hackers.
  • Raising public awareness of ethical hacking can increase trust in cybersecurity professionals and reduce vulnerability to attacks.

WAN Design & Raspberry Pi Supercomputer

CSC 395 · Hampton University · Cisco Packet Tracer · Raspberry Pi · Linux · SSH

This section holds the two projects I completed simultaneously in my Networking course: designing a functional Wide Area Network (WAN) topology in Cisco Packet Tracer, and building a functional Raspberry Pi LAN cluster that demonstrated distributed computing, routing, and collaborative file management.

WAN Design (Cisco Packet Tracer)

  • Designed a multi-branch WAN topology with routers, switches, wireless access points, and end devices across two branches.
  • Configured routing protocols, subnetting, and network security measures.
  • Simulated real-world enterprise network scenarios including server-client communication across branches.

RPi LAN Cluster

  • Built a 5-node Raspberry Pi LAN using a star topology with an unmanaged Ethernet switch.
  • Assigned static IPv4 addresses and configured hostnames for each node (Pi-Master, Node1–Node4).
  • Enabled passwordless SSH across all nodes using RSA key pairs for secure inter-node communication.
  • Implemented collaborative file sharing via SSH as a substitute for Samba after troubleshooting installation issues.
  • Served as Node 4 (Child) in the cluster hierarchy, contributing to setup, configuration, and testing.

Skills Demonstrated

  • Linux CLI configuration · SSH setup and key management · Hardware integration

Parallel Edge-Telemetry: Pi-to-Pi-to-Pi

CSC 571 · Hampton University · Raspberry Pi · Python · MPI · Oscilloscope · CRC

This project helped demonstrate key concepts in Data Communications & Networking by focusing on designing and implementing a small parallel computing system using Raspberry Pis. The system demonstrated how computers communicate using electrical signals, how noise impacts transmissions, and how distributed devices can work together to process data. Additionally, it allowed me to showcase and build my leadership & project management skills.

What We Built

  • Networked 3 Raspberry Pi 4 units (piTX, piRX, piCL1) with static IPs, SSH, and passwordless key authentication.
  • Implemented an LED-based digital transmitter using GPIO17 on a breadboard circuit with the Adeept breakout board.
  • Performed oscilloscope-based timing analysis to measure signal quality and voltage waveforms.
  • Applied data framing and CRC validation to ensure transmission accuracy across nodes.
  • Configured MPI (mpich + mpi4py) across all nodes for distributed computing communication.

Technical Stack

  • Raspberry Pi OS Lite · Python 3 · mpi4py · matplotlib · gpiozero · Wireshark · iperf3
  • ABestop AT312G oscilloscope for signal analysis · Breadboard + LEDs + resistors for hardware circuit

Key Outcomes

  • Successfully demonstrated physical-layer signal generation, timing analysis, and multi-node MPI communication.
  • Gained deep hands-on experience with the intersection of hardware and distributed software systems.
  • Showcased leadership by coordinating a team of 5, managing project timelines, and ensuring successful delivery of all components.
